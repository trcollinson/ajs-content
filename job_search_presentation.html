üéØ The Right Job vs. The Dream Job

Key Insight:

Targeting the "right" opportunity often leads to your dream job faster than applying directly for dream jobs.

‚ùå The Dream Job Trap:

  • Applying to positions 2-3 levels above your experience
  • Competing with 500+ other applicants
  • Getting frustrated with constant rejections
  • Thinking it's just a "numbers game"

‚úÖ The Right Job Strategy:

  • Target roles where you meet 80%+ of requirements
  • Get hired and prove your worth quickly
  • Build internal relationships and reputation
  • Move up faster from the inside

Real Example: A developer coming straight out of college who has just college experience and a lot of college projects applying for roles making $80k and sometimes well into the 6 figures because their college said "they are worth it" had no luck. After targeting entry-level positions and proving himself, he received multiple interview requests within 2 weeks.

üîç Reading Job Requirements Like a Decoder

Learning to Separate "Must Have" from "Nice to Have"

üö® Red Flags - You're Not Ready:

  • "5+ years in [specific industry]" - when you have 0
  • "Expert level" or "Subject Matter Expert"
  • Long list of very specific technical requirements
  • Regulatory/compliance experience when it's critical

‚úÖ Green Lights - You Can Apply:

  • "3+ years experience" - often means 1-2 will work
  • "Familiar with" vs. "Expert in"
  • "Preferred" or "Bonus points for"
  • Skills you can learn quickly on the job

The 11:59 Over-Extension Example:

A candidate applied claiming IIS (Immunization Information System) subject matter experience and rated their C/C++ skills as 9 out of 10. They got the interview, but when answering technical questions, it became obvious they had overstated their qualifications. The interview ended early - honesty in self-assessment is crucial.

Pro Tip: If you meet 70-80% of the requirements and the missing pieces are learnable, apply. Companies often hire for potential, not perfection.

üîÑ The Experience Translation Problem

The Hard Truth:

Your skills don't automatically equal seniority in a new field or industry.

Why This Happens:

  • Industry Context Matters: 15 years in manufacturing ‚â† 15 years in fintech
  • Technology Evolution: Your React skills from 2019 need updating
  • Company Culture: Each organization has its own way of doing things
  • Stakeholder Relationships: You need to rebuild your professional network

‚ùå What Doesn't Work:

  • Assuming your title transfers directly
  • Only highlighting years of experience
  • Expecting to start at the same level immediately
  • Dismissing "entry-level" opportunities

‚úÖ What Works:

  • Focus on transferable skills
  • Show willingness to learn industry specifics
  • Consider lateral or slight step-back moves
  • Emphasize your ability to ramp up quickly

Remember: Taking a strategic step back often leads to faster advancement than struggling to maintain an unsustainable level.

üì± Your Three-Message Consistency Check

The Golden Rule:

Your LinkedIn profile, resume, and interview story must tell the same level story.

The Three Channels That Must Align:

1. üíº LinkedIn Profile

  • Headline: Describe skills you bring, not the title you want
  • Summary: Match the level you're actually targeting
  • Experience: Emphasize relevant skills for your target role

2. üìÑ Resume

  • Mirror the language from job postings you're applying to
  • Quantify achievements at the appropriate level
  • Remove or de-emphasize experience that suggests overqualification

3. üé§ Interview Story

  • Explain your career transition confidently
  • Show enthusiasm for the level you're targeting
  • Avoid language that suggests you're "settling"

Real Problem: A talented developer with strong coding abilities convinced himself he should be "in management." Since he lacked development management experience, he pivoted his messaging toward project management because it seemed "easier to explain in interviews." His LinkedIn highlighted PM skills, his resume emphasized coordination over coding, and his interview stories focused on meetings and timelines rather than technical achievements. Result? He didn't land any positions because employers couldn't tell if he wanted to code or manage - his mixed messaging made him appear unfocused and unclear about his career direction.

ü§ù When to Network Up vs. Apply Direct

üéØ Direct Applications Work For:

  • Entry to Mid-Level Roles
  • Individual contributor positions
  • Clear skill requirements you meet
  • High-volume hiring (junior developers, analysts)
  • Companies with structured recruiting processes

ü§ù Networking Essential For:

  • Director+ Level Positions
  • VP, AVP, Senior Manager roles
  • Career pivots into new industries
  • Competitive, relationship-based fields
  • Small companies or specialized roles

The Networking Strategy for Senior Roles:

Before applying, conduct informational interviews to understand:

  • Where your experience would fit in their organization
  • What level entry point makes sense for your background
  • Who the key decision-makers are
  • What the company culture and expectations really are

Why This Matters: At senior levels, companies aren't just hiring skills - they're hiring judgment, leadership, and cultural fit. These qualities are nearly impossible to assess from a resume alone.

‚ö° The 'Paying Your Dues' Strategy

The Counter-Intuitive Truth:

Sometimes taking a step back leads to faster advancement than trying to maintain an unsustainable level.

When This Strategy Works Best:

  • You're changing industries or functions
  • You have skills but lack recent, relevant experience
  • You've been unemployed for an extended period
  • You need to rebuild your professional credibility

‚ùå How NOT to Frame It:

  • "I'm willing to take a step down"
  • "I know this is below my level, but..."
  • "I'm overqualified, but I need a job"
  • "This is temporary until something better opens up"

‚úÖ How to Frame It Positively:

  • "I'm excited to get hands-on with..."
  • "This role lets me focus on what I love most..."
  • "I want to learn your industry from the ground up"
  • "I see this as an investment in my long-term growth"

The Construction-to-Code Success Story:

A general contractor with concrete experience taught himself programming while supporting his family. Initially, he targeted software management positions, thinking his construction leadership would translate - it didn't. Next, he applied for junior developer roles but approached them with resentment about "starting over." When he finally embraced a paid internship with genuine enthusiasm and humility, everything changed. His eagerness to learn and strong work ethic quickly set him apart. Ten years later, he's now a Vice President at a tech company - proving that the right attitude at the right level can lead to extraordinary advancement.

Key Point: Companies can sense when you're genuinely excited about the role versus when you're "settling." Your enthusiasm for the actual work matters more than your previous title.
